Introduction to the Unit

📌 What This Unit Is About

Every day people across the UK use public services. Whether you call 999 in an emergency, visit your GP, or simply put your recycling out – you are using a public service.

In this unit you will find out about the many different public services available in the UK and in your local area. You will research what they do, who provides them, and how they meet the needs of the public.

Learning Aims

Learning Aim A – Find out information about different public services

Learning Aim B – Produce information about public services in your area, showing how they meet the needs of the public

Learning Aim A – Knowledge Hubs

📚 Knowledge Hub A1: Types and Purposes of Public Services

Public services are organisations that provide support and help to people. They are funded by the government or by charities.

Categories of Public Services

CategoryExamplesWhat They Do
Armed ServicesBritish Army, Royal Navy, RAFDefend the UK and support in emergencies
Emergency ServicesPolice, Fire Service, AmbulanceRespond to 999 calls and keep people safe
Health ServicesNHS hospitals, GPs, ParamedicsProvide medical care and treatment
Law and OrderCourts, Prison Service, ProbationMaintain justice and the rule of law
Social ServicesChildren's services, care homesSupport vulnerable people in society
EnvironmentalRefuse collection, parks, recyclingMaintain a clean and safe environment
Leisure ServicesLibraries, sports centres, museumsImprove quality of life for communities

Who Provides Public Services?

ProviderExplanation
Central GovernmentThe UK government funds national services like the NHS and Armed Forces from Westminster.
Local GovernmentYour local council runs services like refuse collection, parks, and libraries in your area.
Regional GovernmentIn Wales, the Senedd is responsible for services like health and education across Wales.
Third SectorCharities and voluntary organisations provide services such as food banks and housing support.

Six Functions of Public Services

FunctionWhat This Means
DefenceProtecting the UK from threats (e.g. the Armed Forces)
ProtectionKeeping individuals safe from harm (e.g. Police, Fire Service)
JusticeInvestigating crime and ensuring consequences (e.g. Courts, Prison)
TransportationMaintaining roads and public transport (e.g. Local Councils)
Health & WellbeingProviding medical care and promoting healthy lifestyles (e.g. NHS)
Maintaining SecurityMonitoring and responding to threats to public safety (e.g. Border Force)
📚 Knowledge Hub A2: Ways of Working in Public Services

Some public services are uniformed. Some are not. Both types carry out essential work.

UniformedNon-Uniformed
ExamplesPolice, Fire, Army, Ambulance, Prison OfficersSocial workers, council staff, NHS managers, charity workers
Why / Why Not?Helps identify them quickly, shows authorityNot required – their role does not need it in the same way

Types of Uniform

TypeExamples
Basic / StandardPolice officer's tunic, paramedic's uniform – worn daily for identification
Specialist / ProtectiveFire kit, HAZCHEM suits, public order suits for riots
CeremonialQueen's Guard bearskin hats, police dress uniform for remembrance parades

Drill and Common Practices

  • Discipline – Following orders precisely is essential in dangerous situations
  • Teamwork – Everyone moves together as one unit
  • Confidence – Practising routines builds self-assurance
  • Standards – Smart appearance and posture reflect pride in the service
📚 Knowledge Hub A3: Research Skills and Sources

To find information about public services, you need to use different sources. A good researcher uses more than one source and checks that information is accurate and up to date.

Source TypeExamplesThings to Check
Websitespolice.uk, gov.wales, nhs.ukIs it official? When was it last updated?
LeafletsFrom police stations, council officesIs it still current? When produced?
Books / JournalsBTEC textbooks, professional magazinesIs the author qualified? Is it recent?
InterviewsSpeaking to a police officer, firefighterPrepare questions; record answers carefully
VisitsFire station, police station, council officesTake notes on what you see and find out
Checking Validity: Is it recent? Is the author official? Does another source agree? Is the source trying to promote a particular point of view?

Learning Aim B – Knowledge Hubs

📚 Knowledge Hub B1: Requirements and Expectations of the Public

Public services exist to serve the public. Different people in the community need different things from public services.

Who Uses Public Services?

Type of UserExampleService They Might Need
Individual CitizensA family needing housing supportSocial housing / local council
Sections of CommunityOlder people needing careCare homes / social services / NHS
Whole CommunitiesA town needing refuse collectedLocal council environmental services

What the Public Expects

  • Value for money – services should be efficient and not wasteful
  • Convenience – services should be accessible and easy to use
  • Quality – services must be professional and effective
  • Timely response – especially in emergencies
  • Recognition of diversity – staff must treat everyone fairly
  • Entitlement – people expect to receive services they have a right to
Remember: The public may not always think about the availability of resources. During a major incident, response times may be longer because all units are deployed elsewhere.
📚 Knowledge Hub B2: Presenting Your Information

When you produce your document for Learning Aim B, you must think about your audience and choose the right format.

FormatGood ForTips
LeafletShort, clear info for the publicHeadings, bullet points, images; simple language
PosterKey facts and eye-catching infoLarge text, clear layout, limited words
ReportDetailed written informationUse sections, paragraphs, and a clear structure
SlidesTalking to an audienceUse images, keep text short, practise

Key Communication Skills

  • Audience awareness – write for the person who will read your document
  • Accuracy – make sure all facts are correct
  • Relevance – only include information related to the topic
  • Legibility – make your document easy to read
  • Organisation – use headings, sections, and a logical order

Glossary of Key Terms

TermDefinition
Armed ServicesThe military forces of the UK: the British Army, Royal Navy, and Royal Air Force.
Central GovernmentThe UK national government based in Westminster, responsible for running the country.
Chain of CommandThe line of authority from senior officers down to junior ranks.
DisciplineFollowing rules, orders, and standards of behaviour.
DrillA set of precise movements and commands practised to build discipline and teamwork.
Emergency ServicesThe police, fire service, and ambulance service – the three main 999 response services.
HAZCHEMHazardous chemicals – specialist equipment and suits used when dealing with dangerous chemical incidents.
Local GovernmentThe council that manages local services such as refuse collection, parks, and libraries.
Non-uniformed ServicesPublic services whose staff do not wear a uniform, such as social workers and council planners.
Public ServiceAn organisation that provides a service to the public, funded by the government or charities.
Regional GovernmentIn Wales, this is the Senedd (Welsh Parliament), which makes decisions about services like health and education in Wales.
Research LogA record of sources used and information found during research.
Secondary SourceInformation gathered from a source not originally created for your specific research, such as a website or book.
Social ServicesA public service that supports vulnerable people including children at risk, older people, and those with disabilities.
Third SectorOrganisations that are not part of the government and are not businesses, such as charities and voluntary groups.
Uniformed ServicesPublic services whose staff wear a recognisable uniform, such as the police, fire service, and armed forces.
Valid SourceA source that is reliable, accurate, and up to date.
